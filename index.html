Alcina in Concert

The adaptation of this opera is intended as a semi-staged collage of materials from George Frideric Handel’s great opera, ‘Alcina.’ It is a chance to enjoy the spectacular arias of this opera with theatrical staging to connect the characters, story, music and drama.

Handel draws upon the colorful characters of Ariosto’s epic poem, ‘Orlando Furioso,’ in this drama of twisted love, passionate lust, disguised personalities, and magical play. Drama and music are woven together to develop this opera seria steeped in mythological knights and sorceress queens, all of whom are vulnerable to their raw emotions and human faults. This concert adaptation invites the enchanting music and gripping drama to bring life to the tale of Handel’s ‘Alcina.’

Synopsis

Scene I

In the opening Recitativo, Bradamante and Melisso have arrived on Alcina's magical island disguised and in search of Bradamante's betrothed, Ruggiero. Alcina welcomes the visitors to her island (Recit: Alcina, Bradamante, Melisso). Alcina then sings of her adoration for Ruggiero, whom she has taken as lover, (Alcina: Di' cor mio). After Alcina's exit, Ruggiero confirms his love for Alcina (unaware it is a spell), and casts off the visitors for challenging his affections (Ruggiero: Di te mi rido). Ruggiero exits in search of Alcina, leaving Bradamante cruelly spurned and confused.

Scene II

Alcina's capricious sister, Morgana approaches Bradamante and sings of her new feelings in her flirtatious aria, (Morgana: O sapre al riso). Following her display of affection for Bradamante, Morgana's lover Oronte becomes angry and confronts Bradamante (Recit: Oronte). Caught between lovers, Bradamante tells Morgana that her feelings are the cause of problems for both of them, and Oronte to calm his jealousy (Bradamante: E gelosia). As a response to Bradamante, Oronte offers some advice that men should never trust women, as inspired by his own lover, Morgana (Oronte: Semplicetto!). Further angering Oronte, Morgana calls after Bradamante to return, and reiterates her new-found love (Morgana: Tornami).

Scene III

As the spell has been lifted thanks to Melisso’s magical ring, Ruggiero sings oh how he can now see Alcina's sorcery (Ruggiero: Qual Portendo). Melisso reminds Ruggiero that in loving Alcina, he has cruelly hurt his true love, Bradamante (Melisso: Pensa a chi). Reality is still dazed, but Ruggiero sings that he feels awful if what Melisso says is true (Ruggiero: Mi lusinga). Alcina hears of Ruggiero's realization, and sings of both her broken heart and feelings of vengeance (Alcina: Ah! Mio cor).

Intermission

Scene IV

Act two will open to find Oronte singing of women's trickery, (Oronte: E un folle). Morgana enters pleading for Oronte to take her back now that Bradamante has been reunited with Ruggiero (Recit: Oronte+Morgana, Aria Morgana: Credete al mio). Oronte then sings that a moment of love is worth more than the pain (Oronte: Un momento di). Despite Morgana’s wandering fidelity, the lovers are reunited.

Scene V

Alcina is furious with Ruggiero and calls him a traitor for abandoning her (Recit: Ruggiero+Alcina, Aria Alcina: Ma quando). Preparing to battle the monsters and sea storms of Alcina’s sorcery, Ruggiero sings (Ruggiero: Sta nell'Ircana). Melisso urges Bradamante to find safety (Recit: Bradamante+Melisso), but Bradamante is unafraid, as true and faithful love will prevail despite Alcina's sorcery (Bradamante: Alma fedel). Alcina, defeated and scorned, sings of cruel Ruggiero and Bradamante, while Bradamante and Ruggiero are reunited and sing of love conquering evil (Trio: Alcina, Ruggiero, Bradamante: Non e amor).

Biographies

Together with Harvey Davies, an accomplished early music specialist, this vibrant team of young emerging artists form an ensemble prepared to tackle the music and the drama of Handel’s opera, ‘Alcina.’

Bryony Williams, Soprano

Currently based in Berlin, Anglo-New Zealand Soprano Bryony Williams has most recently been heard in the UK performing Strauss’ ‘Last Four Songs’ with the Stockport Symphony Orchestra. Previous engagements included a highly publicized tour singing Beethoven’s 9th Symphony with the Orchestre Symphonique de Kimbanguiste from the Democratic Republic of Congo, performing at the Bridgewater Hall in Manchester, Colston Hall in Bristol, and the Royal Festival Hall in London. Bryony has been engaged as member of the Glyndebourne Festival Chorus and tour performing at the Royal Albert Hall and across the UK. During her studies at the Royal Northern College of Music, Bryony was selected for a place on the prestigious ‘International Artist Diploma’ where she performed the role of ‘Adina,’ in Donizetti’s ‘L’elisir d’amore.’ Her studies at the RNCM were generously funded by the Sir John Fisher Foundation. Bryony was the winner of both the ‘Frederic Cox Award for Singing’ and the ‘Michael Kennedy Award for the Singing of Strauss.’

Heather Lowe, Mezzo-Soprano

British Mezzo-Soprano Heather Lowe has recently finished at the National Opera Studio in London, studying with Susan Waters. Following this training, Heather has gone on to cover and perform the role of ‘Rosina’ with Opera North and the Welsh National Opera. Future engagements include work with Opera North performing in Humperdinck’s ‘Hansel and Gretel’ and Strauss’ ‘Der Rosenkavalier.’ While studying at the Royal Northern College of Music Heather performed the roles of Orfeo in Gluck’s ‘Orfeo ed Euridice,’ Penelope in Monteverdi’s ‘Il Ritorno d’Ulisse in Patria,’ and Florence Pike in Britten’s ‘Albert Herring.’ For the British Youth Opera Heather sang Fidalma in Cimarosa’s ‘The Secret Marriage.’ Heather was a member of the chorus for Glyndebourne Festival Opera 2014 in Eugene Onegin, Don Giovanni and La Traviata. Last September she covered the role of 2nd Witch and Dama Macbeth for Scottish Opera. Recent performances included singing the role of ‘An Apprentice’ in Wagner’s ‘Die Meistersinger’ with the Glyndebourne Festival and singing Tisbe in Holland Park’s production of ‘Cenerentola.’

'Mi lusinga il dolce affetto'

Joanna Norman, Soprano

Australian Soprano, Joanna Norman has recently sung the title role of Gretel in Humperdinck’s ‘Hansel und Gretel’ with the Orkney Festival in Scotland. She is currently studying at the Alexander Gibson Opera School at the Conservatoire of Scotland in Glasgow. Previously she completed her masters at the Royal Northern College of Music where she sang the roles of Tytania in Britten’s ‘A Midsummer Night’s Dream,’ Euridice in Gluck’s ‘Orfeo ed Euridice,’ and Adina in Donizetti’s ‘L’elisir d’amore.’ Her recent performances include work with the Buxton Music Festival, covering Euridice in Gluck’s Orfeo ed Euridice, Lucia, Lucia di Lammermoor, and performing Camille and Le Petite Chiffoniere in Charpentier’s Louise. For the Royal Conservatoire of Scotland, Joanna has recently performed the role of Despina in Mozart’s ‘Cosi fan Tutte.’ Joanna is the recipient of the Dr. V. H Cooper Memorial Prize and the Edith Cowan Prize in Music for musical excellence and the Prichard-Panizza Music Travel Scholarship and UWA Music Travel Scholarship.

'Tornami a vagheggiar'

Chase Henry Hopkins, Tenor

American Tenor, Chase Henry Hopkins is currently completing his studies at the Royal Conservatoire of Scotland having trained at the Dutch National Opera Academy and completed his masters at the Royal Northern College of Music. Recent performances include singing the role of Don Basilio in Mozart’s ‘Le Nozze di Figaro’ at the Royaumont Festival in Paris with Rene Jacobs. Chase’s recent concert work included the Mozart Requiem, Mozart Mass in C Minor, and Bach’s St. John Passion. Chase has worked in the ensemble of the Festival d’Aix-en-Provence in France, the Scottish Opera, Grange Park Opera, the Zurich Singer Akadamie at the Zurich Tonehalle, and will sing in the Wexford Festival ensemble this fall. While completing his studies at the Royal Northern College of Music, Chase performed the roles of Telemaco in Monteverdi’s ‘Il Ritorno d’Ulisse in Patria,’ and Nemorino in Donizetti’s ‘L’elisir d’amore.’ Previous roles include Don Ottavio in Mozart’s ‘Don Giovanni,’ and Jupitoer in Handel’s ‘Semele,’ with the Jackdaw’s Young Artists.

Elizabeth Humphries, Soprano

Canadian Soprano, Elizabeth Humphries completed her masters and post-graduate artist’s diploma at the Royal Norethern College of Music where she attended on a full scholarship, supported by the John Zachonis Fund. Recently performed works on the Oratorio stage include Handel’s Messiah and Vivaldi Magnificat for Glossop Choral Society, Vivaldi’s Gloria for the Northern Lights Symphony Orchestra and Vox Cetera Chamber Choir at St. Martin in the Fields, Mozart's Requiem for Bakewell Choral Society, and Beethoven Mass in C for the Lancaster Singers. Elizabeth enjoyed several roles at the RNCM such as Melanto in Monteverdi's "Il Ritorno d'Ulisse in Patria", Mathurine is Gluck's "The Drunkard Cured", (both conducted by Roger Hamilton) and Amy in Sondheim's "Company"(conducted by Julian Kelly). Elizabeth’s passion for musical theatre culminated in a well received cabaret-style show for the Greater Manchester Fringe Festival entitled “Climbing Uphill” which was totally conceived and performed by Elizabeth and two colleagues from the RNCM. She has recently covered the role of Mrs. Grose in Britten’s “The Turn of the Screw” for Manchester Opera Ensemble and has just finished performing the roles of Nikolaus and Printmaster in Kevin Malone’s “Mysterious 44” – a new opera directed by Caroline Clegg and filmed for television.

Benjamin Kevin Lewis, Baritone

Benjamin Lewis is a baritone who began singing as a founding member of the Hallé Youth Choir and a student of Patrick McGuigan. His roles have included Buonafede (Il Mondo della Luna), Peter (Hansel and Gretel), Sharpless (Madama Butterfly), Il Conte (Le Nozze di Figaro), and whilst in the 2014 Buxton Festival Chorus he also covered Count Harasova (The Jacobin). He appeared in three RNCM operas, as Boris (Paradise Moscow), Belcore (L'Elisir d'Amore), and Danilo (The Merry Widow). In 2015 he was a member of the Glyndebourne chorus. Benjamin is also a recitalist, most often in partnership with accompanist Eda Seppar, performing songs by Schumann, Brahms, Loewe, Ibert, Rimsky-Korsakov, Vaughan-Williams, and Butterworth. Benjamin was the winner of the 2014 Frederic Cox Award, the 2015 Elizabeth Harwood Memorial Award, the Judges’ Discretionary Prize for ‘showing huge promise as a Verdi Baritone’ at the inaugural Fulham Opera Verdi Prize in 2015, and was awarded the 2015 Silver Medal by the Worshipful Company of Musicians. Currently tutored by Nicholas Powell, Benjamin is kindly funded by the Headley Trust, the Help Musicians Fleming Award, the Help Musicians Postgraduate Performance Award, the Tillett Trust and the Colin Keer Trust. He is a recipient of an Independent Opera Postgraduate Voice Fellowship, and is a Samling Artist.

Harvey Davies, Piano

Harvey Davies began playing the piano before he was three. He was a chorister at New College, Oxford, before attending Marlborough College with a top Music Scholarship. Harvey studied with Helen Davies and David Parkhouse before going to Ryszard Bakst at the Royal Northern College of Music from 1983 to 1987. In 1990 Harvey gave his South Bank debut during the Park Lane Group’s prestigious contemporary music series and has performed at most of the UK’s significant venues. Harvey is established as a fine chamber musician and accompanist. He has worked with many eminent musicians including Atar Arad, Alison Balsom, Alfie Boe, Rebecca Evans, Catrin Finch, Evelyn Glennie, Emma Johnson, Guy Johnston, and Jennifer Pike. Harvey runs a chamber music series in Manchester and Wilmslow with his exciting new group ‘The Pleyel Ensemble.’ Comprising some of the UK’s leading instrumentalists the Pleyels are indulging their interests in performing lesser-known 18th century as well as British chamber music and are actively commissioning new music. Harvey is the pianist in Ensemble Cymru. They were nominated for a Royal Philharmonic Society Award in 2006 and continue to receive acclaim for their innovative work in education as well as their performances.

Harvey has recently been appointed as Teaching Fellow in Historical Performance at the Royal Northern College of Music where he is also a staff pianist. He is regularly an artist at the Two Moors Festival, and was for ten years official pianist at the National Eisteddfod of Wales and an ABRSM examiner. He lives in Manchester with his wife, the cellist Heather Bills.
